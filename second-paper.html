1. Machine Drawing

Explanation: Machine drawing is a fundamental skill in mechanical engineering, involving the creation of detailed technical drawings of machine parts. It includes orthographic projections (2D views like front, top, and side), isometric drawings (3D representations), and sectional views to show internal features. The topic also covers drawing joints—permanent (e.g., welded, riveted) and temporary (e.g., nut-bolt)—used to assemble machine components. Missing views test spatial visualization, requiring you to deduce a third view from two given projections (e.g., deriving the side view from front and top views). Dimensioning ensures that drawings are precise and usable for manufacturing. This skill is critical for designing, assembling, and manufacturing mechanical components, ensuring they fit together correctly and function as intended in real-world applications like engines or industrial machinery.

  • Finding missing views from two given projections and dimensioning:
    • Missing views of prismatic workpieces
    • Missing views of cylindrical workpieces
    • Missing views of pyramidal, conical, cylindrical cut workpieces
  • Isometric drawing of machine parts (including sections)
  • Drawing of joints:
    • Permanent joints
    • Temporary joints
    • Drawing exercises: Nut-bolt/threaded joints, riveted joints, welded joints and symbols, gears, keys, spline joints
    • Orthographic projection

Sample Questions:

  • Question 1 (4 marks): A prismatic workpiece has a front view showing a square of side 60 mm with a 20 mm diameter circular hole at its center, and the top view shows a rectangle of 60 mm × 40 mm. Draw the missing side view, ensuring all dimensions are clearly marked. Explain briefly how you determined the position of the circular hole in the side view, considering the principles of orthographic projection.
  • Question 2 (4 marks): Differentiate between a single-riveted lap joint and a double-riveted lap joint in terms of their construction and strength. Provide a detailed sketch of both joints, labeling the rivets, plates, and overlap. Discuss one practical application of each joint in mechanical assemblies, such as in pressure vessels or structural frameworks.
  • Question 3 (4 marks): Draw the welding symbols for a fillet weld and a groove weld as per standard conventions. Explain the components of each symbol (e.g., arrow, reference line, weld type) and describe their applications in machine assembly. For instance, mention how a fillet weld might be used in joining two perpendicular plates in a machine frame, and how a groove weld is used in heavy-duty structures like pipelines.
  • Question 4 (8 marks): A cylindrical workpiece has a diameter of 50 mm and a height of 80 mm, with a 30 mm deep cylindrical cut (diameter 25 mm) on its top face. Draw the isometric view of this workpiece, including a sectional view through the cylindrical cut to show internal details. Ensure the drawing includes all necessary dimensions and labels. Additionally, explain the steps you followed to construct the isometric view, such as setting up the 30-degree axes, drawing the base circle, and depicting the cut section. Discuss the importance of sectional views in machine drawing for manufacturing purposes, particularly in understanding internal features that are not visible in standard views.
  • Question 5 (8 marks): Draw the orthographic projections (front, top, and side views) of a hexagonal nut with a nominal diameter of 30 mm and a thickness of 15 mm, including a threaded hole of 20 mm diameter. Provide all dimensions, such as the distance across flats (approximately 1.5 times the nominal diameter), the thickness, and the thread details. Label each view clearly and explain the importance of orthographic projections in ensuring accurate manufacturing of machine components like nuts and bolts. Discuss how such drawings are used in a workshop to guide the machining process, ensuring the nut fits perfectly with a corresponding bolt in an assembly like a car engine or a mechanical press.

2. Heat Engines

Explanation: Heat engines convert thermal energy into mechanical work, a core concept in mechanical engineering. They operate on thermodynamic cycles like Carnot, Rankine (steam engines), Otto (petrol engines), and Brayton (jet engines). Steam engines use steam pressure to drive pistons, while automotive engines (e.g., petrol, diesel) rely on internal combustion of fuel. Key differences include fuel type (coal/steam vs. gasoline/diesel), efficiency (steam engines are less efficient due to heat loss), and application (steam for locomotives, automotive for vehicles). In aviation, civil aircraft use jet engines (turbojets, turbofans) or turboprops, which operate on the Brayton cycle, providing high power-to-weight ratios. Understanding heat engines is essential for designing efficient power generation systems, optimizing fuel use, and reducing emissions in applications like power plants, vehicles, and aircraft propulsion systems.

  • Different types of heat engines
  • Different cycles involved in heat engines
  • Basic difference in steam engine and automotive engines
  • Different types of power plants (engines) used in civil aircraft

  • Question 2 (4 marks): Compare the Rankine cycle (used in steam engines) with the Otto cycle (used in petrol engines) in terms of their thermodynamic processes. 3. Thermodynamics

Explanation: Thermodynamics studies the relationships between heat, work, and energy in systems, a cornerstone of mechanical engineering. It starts with gas laws (Boyle’s, Charles’, combined) to understand gas behavior under varying pressure, volume, and temperature. The First Law (energy conservation, ΔU = Q - W) explains how heat added to a system either increases internal energy or does work, while the Second Law introduces efficiency limits (e.g., Carnot efficiency) and concepts like entropy (disorder). Thermodynamic properties like enthalpy (H = U + PV) and specific heats (Cv, Cp) are crucial for analyzing processes. Basic processes include constant volume (no work), constant pressure (work = PΔV), isothermal (constant temperature), adiabatic (no heat transfer), and polytropic (generalized process). Engine cycles (Otto for petrol, Diesel for diesel) apply these principles to real-world engines, determining their efficiency and performance in vehicles and power generation systems.

  • General:
    • Boyle's law (P ∝ 1/V), Charles' law (V ∝ T), combined gas law (PV/T = constant)
    • Characteristics of gas constant (R = 8.314 J/mol·K for ideal gases)
    • Terms used in thermodynamics (e.g., system, boundary, state)
  • First Law of Thermodynamics:
    • Definition: Energy cannot be created or destroyed (ΔU = Q - W)
    • Total internal energy (sum of kinetic and potential energies)
    • Mechanical equivalent of heat engine (J = 4.186 J/cal)
  • Second Law of Thermodynamics:
    • Definition: Heat cannot flow from a colder to a hotter body without work (Clausius statement)
    • Thermal efficiency of heat engine (η = 1 - Q2/Q1)
  • Thermodynamic Properties of Fluid (Definitions only):
    • Internal energy (energy within the system)
    • Enthalpy (H = U + PV)
    • Entropy (measure of disorder)
    • Specific heat at constant volume (Cv)
    • Specific heat at constant pressure (Cp)
  • Basic Thermodynamic Processes:
    • Constant volume process (V = constant, W = 0)
    • Constant pressure process (P = constant, W = PΔV)
    • Constant temperature process (T = constant, PV = constant)
    • Adiabatic process (Q = 0, PV^γ = constant)
    • Polytropic process (PV^n = constant)
  • Petrol and Diesel Engine Cycles:
    • Constant volume cycle (Otto cycle for petrol engines)
    • Constant pressure cycle (Diesel cycle for diesel engines)
    • Mixed cycle (dual cycle combining both)

Sample Questions:

  • Question 1 (4 marks): A gas undergoes a constant pressure process where its volume changes from 2 m³ to 5 m³ at a pressure of 150 kPa. Calculate the work done during the process using the formula W = PΔV. Show all steps, including the calculation of ΔV (change in volume) and the final work done in joules (since 1 kPa·m³ = 1000 J). Discuss the significance of this work in a practical application, such as the expansion of gas in a piston-cylinder system used in an engine, and explain how the constant pressure process differs from a constant volume process in terms of work output.

Provide an example of entropy increase in a real-world scenario, such as the cooling of exhaust gases in a car engine, and explain how engineers use the concept of entropy to optimize engine performance by minimizing irreversible losses.
  • Question 3 (4 marks): Compare an adiabatic process with an isothermal process in terms of their thermodynamic behavior. For an adiabatic process, explain that no heat is transferred (Q = 0) and the relationship PV^γ = constant applies, while for an isothermal process, temperature remains constant (PV = constant). Provide one example of each in a heat engine: adiabatic compression in a diesel engine and isothermal expansion in a Stirling engine. Discuss the practical implications of these processes in engine design, such as how adiabatic processes lead to temperature changes that affect efficiency.
  • Question 4 (8 marks): A petrol engine operates on the Otto cycle, commonly used in car engines. Draw the P-V diagram for the Otto cycle, labeling each process: intake stroke (air-fuel mixture enters), compression (adiabatic, volume decreases), combustion (constant volume heat addition), expansion (adiabatic, volume increases), and exhaust stroke (expels gases). Explain each process in detail, focusing on the thermodynamic changes (e.g., pressure and temperature increase during compression).   • Question 5 (8 marks): A heat engine absorbs 1200 kJ of heat from a hot reservoir at 700 K and rejects 500 kJ to a cold reservoir at 350 K. Calculate the following: (a) The thermal efficiency of the engine using the formula η = 1 - Q2/Q1, where Q1 is the heat absorbed and Q2 is the heat rejected. (b) The maximum possible efficiency (Carnot efficiency) using the formula η = 1 - T2/T1, where T1 and T2 are the temperatures of the hot and cold reservoirs in Kelvin. (c) Compare the two efficiencies and explain why the engine’s efficiency is lower than the Carnot efficiency, considering factors like irreversibilities (e.g., friction, heat loss). Discuss how this analysis helps engineers design more efficient heat engines, such as by improving insulation or using higher temperature reservoirs, and provide an example of a practical application, such as a power plant turbine.

4. Basic Industrial Management

Explanation: Industrial management in mechanical engineering focuses on managing resources, workers, and processes in industrial settings. Labour laws (e.g., Nepal Labour Act) ensure fair wages, working hours, and safety standards. Union rights, like collective bargaining, allow workers to negotiate better conditions. Wages and compensation include minimum wage, overtime pay, and benefits, ensuring worker satisfaction. Labour-management relations aim to resolve conflicts and improve productivity through communication and mediation. The International Labour Organization (ILO) sets global labour standards, while the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) ensures aviation safety, relevant for mechanical engineers in aerospace. Industrial hygiene and safety involve practices like using PPE and ensuring proper ventilation to prevent accidents in workshops. This topic ensures engineers can manage teams, comply with regulations, and maintain safe, efficient workplaces.

  • Industrial hygiene and safety

5. Basic Knowledge of Electro-Mechanical Principles

Explanation: Electro-mechanical principles involve the interaction of electrical and mechanical systems, focusing on motors and generators. AC motors (e.g., induction motors) operate on alternating current, using electromagnetic induction to produce rotation, commonly used in pumps and fans. DC motors use direct current and a commutator to convert electrical energy into mechanical motion, ideal for precise control in applications like robotics. Generators convert mechanical energy into electrical energy, using principles like Faraday’s law of electromagnetic induction, as seen in alternators in power plants. This topic is crucial for mechanical engineers to design and maintain systems like conveyor belts, electric vehicles, and power generation units, ensuring efficient energy conversion and reliable operation in industrial settings.

  • Basic knowledge of AC and DC motors
  • Basic knowledge of generators

Discuss the advantages of each: commutators allow precise speed control in DC motors (e.g., in electric trains), while slip rings enable higher efficiency in AC motors (e.g., in industrial fans). Provide an example of a mechanical application where a DC motor is preferred over an AC motor, such as in a robotic arm requiring precise movements.
  • Question 2 (4 marks): List three applications of AC motors in mechanical systems and explain their suitability for each application. (c) Universal motors in power tools like drills, due to their high torque and compact size.   • Question 3 (4 marks): Explain the basic principle of a generator using Faraday’s law of electromagnetic induction. Describe how a rotating coil in a magnetic field induces an electromotive force (EMF), given by EMF = -dΦ/dt, where Φ is the magnetic flux.   • Question 4 (8 marks): Compare the construction and working of an AC motor and a DC motor, focusing on their suitability for mechanical applications. For working, explain how AC motors use electromagnetic induction to produce rotation (e.g., a rotating magnetic field induces current in the rotor), while DC motors use a commutator to reverse current direction for continuous rotation. Discuss the advantages of each: AC motors are low-maintenance and efficient for high-power applications like industrial pumps, while DC motors offer precise speed control for applications like robotics. 6. Industrial Boiler

Explanation: Industrial boilers generate steam or hot water for industrial processes, such as power generation or heating. They work by burning fuel (e.g., coal, natural gas) to heat water in a closed vessel, producing steam that drives turbines or heats systems. Common types include fire-tube boilers (hot gases pass through tubes surrounded by water) and water-tube boilers (water flows through tubes heated by external gases), each suited for different pressure and capacity needs. Boiler fuels vary—coal for large-scale plants, natural gas for cleaner operation, and biomass for sustainability. Efficiency (η = heat output/heat input) depends on combustion efficiency, heat transfer, and minimizing losses (e.g., through insulation). Boilers are critical in industries like manufacturing, where steam powers machinery, or in power plants, where they generate electricity for mechanical systems.

  • Basic working principle
  • Common types of boilers
  • Boiler fuels
  • Boiler efficiency

7. Estimating and Costing

Explanation: Estimating and costing involve analyzing the financial aspects of mechanical engineering projects, ensuring profitability and efficiency. Key concepts include profitability (net profit = revenue - costs), break-even point (where total revenue equals total cost), and return on investment (ROI = (net profit/investment) × 100), which measure project viability. Assets (e.g., machinery) and liabilities (e.g., loans) track financial health, while fixed costs (e.g., equipment) and variable costs (e.g., labour) determine total expenditure. Fixed capital (initial investment) and working capital (daily operations) ensure smooth project execution. Depreciation (value loss over time) and amortization (spreading loan payments) affect long-term costs. Elements of cost—material, labour, overhead—are classified to allocate resources efficiently. This topic helps engineers estimate project costs, manage budgets, and ensure financial success in applications like manufacturing plants or construction projects.

  • General:
    • Concept of profitability (net profit = revenue - costs)
    • Break-even point (where total revenue = total cost)
    • Return on investment (ROI = (net profit/investment) × 100)
    • Liability, assets, fixed cost, variable cost, fixed capital, working capital, equity, depreciation, amortization
    • Elements of cost and classification

8. Applied Mechanics

Explanation: Applied mechanics analyzes the behavior of physical systems under forces and motion, critical for mechanical design. Statics deals with systems in equilibrium, such as calculating forces in a coplanar system (e.g., beams in a machine frame), moments (rotational effects), center of gravity (for stability), and friction (e.g., in bearings). Kinematics studies motion without forces, defining speed (distance/time), velocity (speed with direction), acceleration (rate of velocity change), and analyzing rectilinear (straight-line) and curvilinear (curved) motion. Dynamics applies Newton’s laws to moving systems, covering work (force × distance), energy (kinetic and potential), power (work/time), and the relationship between RPM, torque, and power (Power = (2π·RPM·Torque)/60). The law of conservation of energy ensures energy is neither created nor destroyed. This topic is essential for designing stable structures, analyzing machine motion, and optimizing performance in applications like robotics, vehicles, and industrial machinery.

  • Statics:
    • Coplanar system of intersecting forces
    • Coplanar parallel forces, the moment of a force
    • Center of gravity
    • Friction
  • Kinematics:
    • Definition of technical terms: speed, velocity, acceleration, distance traversed, and their units
    • Trajectory of particles, distance, and time
    • Rectilinear motion of a particle
  • Composition of Simple Motion:
    • Curvilinear motion of a particle
    • Simple motion of a solid body
  • Dynamics:
    • Fundamental laws of dynamics: Newton's laws of motion
    • Work, energy, and power
    • Mechanical energy
    • Relation between RPM, torque, and power
    • Law of conservation of energy

Sample Questions:

  • Question 1 (4 marks): A block of mass 15 kg rests on a surface with a coefficient of friction of 0.4. A horizontal force of 50 N is applied to the block. Calculate the frictional force using the formula Frictional Force = μ·N, where μ is the coefficient of friction and N is the normal force (N = mg). Show all steps: N = 15 kg × 9.81 m/s² = 147.15 N, so Frictional Force = 0.4 × 147.15 = 58.86 N. Since the applied force (50 N) is less than the frictional force, explain that the block does not move, and discuss the significance of friction in a mechanical context, such as in a brake system where friction between the brake pad and disc stops a machine, ensuring safety in a workshop producing mechanical components.
  • Question 2 (4 marks): Define the center of gravity and explain its importance in designing a stable mechanical structure. Describe how the center of gravity is the point where the weight of an object can be considered to act, and how a lower center of gravity improves stability (e.g., in a crane). Discuss its application in a mechanical context, such as designing a robotic arm where the center of gravity must be kept low to prevent tipping during operation. Provide an example of a design failure due to a high center of gravity, such as a poorly balanced machine that topples during operation, and explain how engineers adjust the design (e.g., adding a counterweight) to ensure stability.
  • Question 3 (4 marks): A particle moves with an initial velocity of 4 m/s and accelerates at 3 m/s² for 5 seconds. Calculate the distance traversed using the equation of motion \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \), where u is the initial velocity, a is the acceleration, and t is the time. Show all steps: u = 4 m/s, a = 3 m/s², t = 5 s, so \( s = (4 \times 5) + \frac{1}{2}(3 \times 5^2) = 20 + \frac{1}{2}(3 \times 25) = 20 + 37.5 = 57.5 \) meters. Discuss the significance of this calculation in a mechanical application, such as determining the distance a conveyor belt motor moves a component in a factory, and explain how understanding rectilinear motion helps in optimizing the motor’s speed and timing for efficient production.
  • Question 4 (8 marks): A motor operates at 1800 RPM and delivers a torque of 40 Nm in a mechanical system driving a pump. Calculate the power output using the formula \( \text{Power} = \frac{2\pi \cdot \text{RPM} \cdot \text{Torque}}{60} \). Show all steps: RPM = 1800, Torque = 40 Nm, so Power = (2 × 3.14 × 1800 × 40) / 60 = 452160 / 60 = 7536 W or 7.536 kW. Discuss two factors that could affect the motor’s power output: (a) Mechanical losses, such as friction in bearings, which can be reduced by using ball bearings and proper lubrication. (b) Electrical losses, such as resistance in the motor windings, which can be minimized by using high-conductivity copper. Explain the significance of this power calculation in a mechanical application, such as ensuring the motor can drive a pump delivering water at a required flow rate in an irrigation system. Conclude with a discussion on how engineers optimize motor performance, such as by selecting the right RPM and torque for the pump’s load requirements, ensuring efficient operation and energy savings.
  • Question 5 (8 marks): A particle follows a curvilinear path under the influence of a force, such as a projectile in a mechanical testing scenario. Derive the equations of motion for the particle, considering its velocity components (tangential and normal) and acceleration. Explain how the tangential velocity determines the speed along the path, while the normal velocity causes the change in direction, and how the total acceleration has a tangential component (changes speed) and a normal component (changes direction). Apply Newton’s second law (F = ma) to analyze the forces, such as gravity acting downward and air resistance opposing motion. Sketch the curvilinear path, showing the particle’s trajectory, velocity vectors (tangential and normal), and forces acting (gravity, air resistance). Discuss the practical application of this analysis in a mechanical context, such as designing a robotic arm that follows a curved path to pick and place components in an assembly line, and explain how understanding curvilinear motion ensures precise control and avoids collisions.
